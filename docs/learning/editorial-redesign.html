The editorial reading design system

Asterion’s macOS interface is a cover-led library with a quiet detail pane and a separate long-form reader. It uses real catalog art as the visual focus and reserves interface color for selection, progress, and the primary reading action.

Research that shaped the plan

  • Apple Books treats covers, curated shelves, and “pick up where you left off” as the core library hierarchy.
  • Apple Books on Mac exposes font, background, spacing, justification, and column controls for reading comfort.
  • Readwise Reader keeps reading customizable and keyboard-friendly while separating the library from the focused document surface.
  • Matter’s reading themes use lower-contrast paper and sepia palettes to reduce glare.
  • Apple’s color guidance recommends consistent semantic use, appearance-aware contrast, and testing under different displays and lighting.
  • Material’s color roles separate accent, surface, container, and on-surface colors instead of treating every background as a brand color.
  • WCAG 2.2 sets a 4.5:1 minimum for normal text and 3:1 for essential interface boundaries.
  • Apple’s typography guidance identifies SF Pro as the native macOS UI face, recommends regular-to-bold weights, and advises minimizing the number of typefaces.
  • Literata was created for Google Play Books and optimized for long-form reading across screen sizes; Asterion bundles its variable roman and italic files under the SIL Open Font License.

Implemented design plan

  1. Replace the dense novel list with responsive Featured, Continue Reading, and Trending shelves.
  2. Keep the sidebar stable and native to macOS while giving selection a restrained oxblood treatment.
  3. Reduce the detail pane to cover, title, essential metadata, one primary reading action, synopsis, and five recent chapters.
  4. Use a warm paper surface and a literary serif for long-form text; use the system sans serif for controls and metadata.
  5. Keep the reader in its own window with adjustable text size, spacing, and measure.
  6. Preserve real loading, empty, authentication, progress, and failure states.
  7. Make Profile a reading dashboard: identity and activity occupy the main pane, while account management and sync status stay in the inspector.
  8. Keep every content surface scrollable with trackpad, mouse wheel, and keyboard while hiding persistent scroll indicators across the app.
  9. Keep primary navigation visible in both modes: the sidebar compacts to a narrow icon rail instead of disappearing or surrendering its navigation state.
  10. Label reading progress with the reader’s current chapter number and title, not only a percentage.
  11. Open each novel with a sentence-aware synopsis preview; reveal the complete synopsis only on request.

System map

  • Theme.swift owns the Graphite & Crimson colors, Literata display and reading roles, SF Pro utility text, and bundled-font registration.
  • Motion.swift owns the shared press, hover, reveal, and navigation timing plus Reduce Motion behavior.
  • SidebarView owns app-area navigation, signed-in identity, and the expanded-to-icon-rail compact mode; SwiftUI owns compact state while a narrow AppKit window-frame adjustment preserves the reading workspace.
  • Resources/Brand/AsterionMark.png is the generated celestial-book symbol used by the sidebar lockup; Literata keeps the wordmark editable and sharp at every scale.
  • Resources/AppIcon.icns is the Finder icon: the same celestial-book symbol, placed on the Graphite and Crimson app-icon field. The packaging script emits dist/Asterion.app and removes the obsolete bundle.
  • EditorialCatalogView owns cover shelves, rankings, library grids, selection, and progress tiles.
  • NovelDetailView owns the selected story’s reading action, metadata, collapsible synopsis preview, and recent chapters.
  • AccountSummaryView owns the reader identity, library metrics, active stories, and direct Continue actions; AccountView owns Clerk profile management, sync status, and sign-out. AsterionAuthenticationView hosts Clerk sign-in in a dedicated, correctly sized macOS window.
  • ScrollIndicatorHider is the narrow AppKit bridge that disables NSScrollView scrollers even when macOS is configured to always show them; SwiftUI still owns every scrolling surface.
  • ReaderView owns the distraction-free reading window and its per-user typography preferences.
  • AppModel owns shelf data, server progress, and the distinction between chapter metadata and full chapter content.

Graphite anchors navigation, cool gray separates the workspace from white content surfaces, Ink and Slate own text hierarchy, and the logo’s crimson is the only interactive accent. The palette contains no yellow, cream, blue, or competing brand color. Primary text, accent text, accent buttons, sidebar text, and sidebar supporting text exceed enhanced 7:1 contrast; Slate on white remains above 6.7:1 and comfortably exceeds AA.

Typography pairing

Literata is the app’s primary voice: brand, navigation labels, headings, book titles, profile identity, synopsis, and reader chapters. Its variable weight and optical-size support let the same family move from compact UI titles to sustained reading without changing character.

SF Pro remains only for small metadata, toolbar controls, buttons, status labels, and other platform utility text where native macOS density matters most.

Motion language

Asterion uses motion to clarify local state rather than animate whole reading surfaces. Buttons compress for 120 ms, interactive covers lift for 160 ms, selection outlines settle over 220 ms, the sidebar highlight follows navigation with a restrained spring, and the Save symbol morphs in place. Catalog, detail, profile, and reader containers remain spatially stable so animation cannot disturb scrolling or text.

Every custom transform reads macOS Reduce Motion. When enabled, hover lift and press scaling are removed while each final state remains immediate and complete. The main split view owns an explicit .all visibility state, and the system collapse command is removed. A custom compact control narrows the window and sidebar together, preserving navigation without allowing the detail workspace to unpredictably consume the released width.
